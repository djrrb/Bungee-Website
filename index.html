Stack

Bungee is a typeface that celebrates the urban sign. From crummy liquor stores to majestic theaters, our cities are packed with vertical signage that stacks Latin letters, one on top of the other, in order to make dramatic use of limited space. Bungee has a specially-drawn vertical-use alphabet that will take your text in a new direction.

Letâ€™s get one thing straight: setting the Latin alphabet vertically is not always a great idea. The Latin alphabet is designed for horizontal writing, and readability experts would tell you to leave verticality to scripts like Chinese or Korean. But in display typography, readability isn't everything. When used deftly and sparingly, stacked letters can help your words stand out among the crowd.

In vertical type, the downward flow of text takes precedence over than the proportions of any individual letter. In Bungee, round characters like O and diagonal characters like A have straight sides, reinforcing this flow and giving each word a well-defined left and right edge. Letters that are unusually wide, narrow, or asymmetric (such as I, L, M, and W) must receive special care.

Bungee implements its vertical forms with OpenType features that are more commonly used for vertical text in East Asian scripts. These are activated by Vertical Type tools in some desktop design applications, or using CSS writing-mode: vertical-rl and text-orientation: upright.

I began drawing Bungee by rotating it rotated 90Â° clockwise, which led to a host of interesting design challenges. I offer this special Rotated version for applications that cannot take advantage of Bungeeâ€™s OpenType capabilities. By rotating the text block 90Â° counter-clockwise, you can simulate vertical text in any application.

Color

Chromatic typefaces are nothing new: sets of wood type intended for overprinting were produced as early as 1841, and quickly became a popular way to add visual interest to posters and broadsides. Today we find ourselves in a renaissance of chromatic type, with many incredible digital fonts released each year that come with layers for chromatic typesetting.

Bungee comes with four layers: Regular, Inline, Outline, and Shade. You can mix and match them to create a variety of chromatic effects. In desktop apps, you can style each layer separately, and overlay them when you are finished. On the web, you can use absolutely-positioned content in :before and :after to place the layers, or use bungee.js will do the heavy lifting for you.

Bungee also comes with a basic set of five standalone fonts: Regular, Hairline, Inline, Outline, and Shade. These are black and white combinations of Bungee Layers that can be used in any typesetting software without advanced features. These five fonts are available on Google Fonts.

Bungee is also available in 3 new color font formats: SVG (Mozilla), COLR/CPAL (Microsoft) and SBIX (Apple). These formats store color information in the font itself, allowing for effortless chromatic typesetting in even the most basic of applications. Support for these formats is far from universal, but I am excited about all of the possibilities that color fonts have to offer. I am hopeful that Bungee Color can help users and developers experiment with these formats.

Bungeeâ€™s stylistic alternates include simplified Deco forms, unserifed forms, and vertical ligatures for double-II and Dutch Ä². Bungee also ships with a large set of arrows, pointing fists, and background shapes that are inspired by the shapes and decorations on old commercial signs. Some of these background shapes will connect to form decorative banners, while other block shapes are meant to encircle a single letter.
