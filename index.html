Fonts for multi-color and vertical typography

In the crowded urban environment, space for signage is always at a premium. From small liquor stores to majestic theaters, sometimes advertising has nowhere to go but up. Bungee is a font family that celebrates the urban sign: it wrangles the Latin alphabet to work vertically as well as horizontally; with separately glyphs, spacing, and kerning for each orientation. It also comes equipped with chromatic inlines, outlines, shades, and ornaments, making it the perfect toolkit to take your text in a new direction.

Colors

Chromatic typefaces are nothing new: sets of wood type intended for overprinting were produced and marketed as early as 1841 and became a popular way for printers to add visual interest to their posters and broadsides. They became more and more elaborate as the nineteenth century progressed, as epitomized by the 1874 specimen by William H. Page & Co. Chromatic type began to fall out of favor as typesetting became more mechanized in the twentieth century, and until recently, color was not a factor in any of the major digital font formats.

Today we find ourselves in a renaissance of chromatic type, with more chromatic type coming out each year. Just like their wooden forbears, they are by layering fonts on top of one another.

Thanks in large part to the recent popularity of Emoji, color font formats have emerged, with Apple, Microsoft, and Adobe offering their own specifications. Thanks to RoboChrome, I have created versions of Bungee for all of these formats, and I am excited about all of the possibilities that these new formats offer.

Bungee comes with Regular, Inline, Outline, and Shade layers, which you can mix and match to create a variety of styles.

In desktop apps, you can use these layers by styling each layer on a different line and setting the leading to zero. On the web, bungee.js will do the heavy lifting for you, by arranging the text into a series of layered <div>s that you can customize in your stylesheet. Verticals

Even though the Latin alphabet is traditionally written horizontally, vertical signage is a ubiquitous part of the urban landscape. Bungee pays homage to these signs, particularly Art Deco marquees from the 1930s and 40s.

Bungee contains a specially drawn set of caps designed for vertical use, and implements them with OpenType features that are more commonly associated with vertical writing in East Asian scripts. These forms even have vertical proportional spacing and kerning.

Some design applications and browsers can take advantage of Bungee’s vertical capabilities. For those, Bungee offers a special version of Bungee where each character lays on its side. This will simulate vertical text in a 90°-rotated text box. Learn more about how to use Bungee’s vertical forms »

Alternates

Since Bungee is only intended to be used for a few words at a time, a single letterform can make a huge difference. Bungee comes with a handful of alternate characters that can help you fine-tune the look and feel of your text. These include a serifless I and L, a smaller, less-obtrusive apostrophe, and a set of rounded, retro caps that are reminiscent of Art Deco signs. More about Bungee’s alternates »

Symbols

Bungee’s Banner and Block shapes will frame your text. You can construct variable-length banners by employing Bungee’s beginning and ending banner elements. Meanwhile, Bungee’s block shapes are designed to be set independently, encircling a single letter. More about Bungee’s ornaments »

Process

In 2011, a crummy sign for the local liquor store got me thinking about stacked letters, and whether a typeface designed for signage could be optimized for vertical setting. I began to collect examples of vertical signage wherever I could find them, both good and bad.

The first version of Bungee was drawn, spaced, and kerned while rotated on its side, which led to a host of interesting design problems. Over the next several years, Bungee grew from a novelty font into a full-fledged research project, encompassing the aesthetic demands of vertical type and technical ins-and-outs of color and vertical writing modes on the web. The design was honored by the Type Directors Club in 2014.

Thanks

Thanks to support from Google and The Font Bureau, Bungee is released under the SIL Open Font License, meaning that it is completely free and open-source.

I sell most of my fonts commercially, but Bungee is well-suited to a libre license because of its support for emerging typographic features like chromatic layers and vertical text. Bungee was part of Microsoft’s early experiments with color fonts, and I hope that as an open-source typeface, it will spark further experimentation with color fonts and foster further development in this field. More widespread use of color and verticality will only lead to better support for these features in desktop applications and on the web, and I hope that Bungee’s license allows it to be a valuable resource for typographers, designers, and developers (professional and amateur alike).
